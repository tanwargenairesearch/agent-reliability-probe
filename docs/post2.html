Note 2

Most agent failures are the kind you can't reproduce

We've made peace with flaky software tests. We haven't reckoned with flaky agents — where the nondeterminism is the model itself, on every decision.

We've lived with flaky tests for years — the ones that pass, then fail, then pass again. We learned to expect that from race conditions and networks. But an agent's nondeterminism is different: the model samples, so the same prompt can take a different reasoning path on every run. A task isn't simply "solved" or "broken" — it's solved with a probability.

To see how this plays out, I ran two frontier models as customer-service agents across 150 tasks (100 retail, 50 airline), 5 runs each — 1,500 runs in total — and sorted every task into three buckets:

  • ✅ Works every time (5 of 5)
  • ⚠️ Inconsistent — passes some runs, fails others (1–4 of 5)
  • ❌ Fails every time (0 of 5)

Of the tasks that ever failed, 71 were inconsistent and only 23 failed every time. The pattern held across both models and both domains: roughly 3 in 4 failures were the inconsistent kind.

Why the inconsistent kind is the dangerous one

A failure that happens every time is the friendly one. It breaks in your demo, it breaks in QA — you see it, you fix it before launch.

An inconsistent failure is the trap. It passes your demo. It passes QA. You ship with confidence. Then it fails for a real customer on the one run that happens to break — and when you go to reproduce it, it works fine. The failures that are hardest to catch are also the most common.

This is exactly why a single test run — the basis of most accuracy numbers — misses the real risk. The cheapest defense is the most ignored: run your eval set k times, not once, and track how often it succeeds every time.
